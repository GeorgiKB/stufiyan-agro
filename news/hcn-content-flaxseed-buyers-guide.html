Why HCN Content Matters When Sourcing Flaxseed

  1. Home
  2. ›
  3. News
  4. ›
  5. Why HCN Content Matters When Sourcing Flaxseed

TL;DR — Key Points

  • Flaxseed contains cyanogenic glycosides that release HCN when ground or milled.
  • EU law (Regulation 2023/915) sets a limit of 250 mg/kg for bulk flaxseed and 150 mg/kg for consumer-facing products.
  • Yellow/golden flaxseed varieties often test at 200–250 mg/kg — close to the bulk limit.
  • Always require an HCN Certificate of Analysis alongside standard quality parameters.
  • Heat processing (extrusion, microwave roasting) reduces HCN by up to 89%.

Global demand for flaxseed has surged. Valued for its omega-3 fatty acids, dietary fibre, and lignans, flaxseed now appears in everything from bakery products to health supplements and animal feed formulations. But alongside these well-known nutritional benefits sits a less discussed quality parameter that every serious buyer should understand: hydrogen cyanide content.

What Are Cyanogenic Glycosides in Flaxseed?

Flaxseed contains several cyanogenic glycosides (CGs), with linustatin and neolinustatin being the most abundant. A third compound, linamarin, is present in smaller concentrations. These substances are stored naturally within the seed's cellular structure, kept physically separated from the enzymes that would break them down.

The issue arises when the seed's tissue is disrupted — through grinding, milling, crushing, or even chewing. At that point, endogenous beta-glucosidase enzymes come into contact with the glycosides, triggering hydrolysis and releasing hydrogen cyanide (HCN). In intact, whole seeds, virtually no free HCN is present. The moment those seeds are processed, the clock starts.

This distinction between whole and processed flaxseed is not just biochemistry — it is the basis upon which the European Union has built its regulatory framework.

How Much HCN Does Flaxseed Actually Contain?

Raw flaxseed HCN potential varies considerably depending on cultivar, growing conditions, and whether the seed was produced for oil extraction or direct human consumption. Published research reports a broad range:

Feed-grade flaxseed (typical)
~377 mg/kg
Human food-grade flaxseed (typical)
~139 mg/kg
Range across ten cultivars (one study)
124–196 mg/kg
Golden/yellow flaxseed (industry observation)
200–250 mg/kg

Oil-type cultivars tend to show higher cyanogenic glycoside levels than fibre-type cultivars, though there is overlap. Golden flaxseed varieties are particularly notable: industry participants have observed that golden linseed rarely tests below 200 mg/kg — a fact that has significant implications under current EU legislation.

EU Regulatory Framework: The Numbers That Matter

The European Union established binding maximum levels for hydrocyanic acid in flaxseed through Commission Regulation (EU) 2022/1364, which amended the older Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006. These limits were subsequently consolidated into Regulation (EU) 2023/915, which entered into force in May 2023. The HCN limits for linseed have been enforceable since 1 January 2023.

There are two key thresholds that every flaxseed buyer and exporter must know:

Linseed placed on the market in general (bulk / industrial use)
≤ 250 mg/kg
Whole, ground, milled, cracked, or chopped linseed for the final consumer
≤ 150 mg/kg

An exception applies to consumer-facing product: if sold in small quantities with a prominent label warning stating "Only to be used for cooking and baking. Do not consume raw!", the 250 mg/kg limit applies instead of 150 mg/kg.

The rationale behind these two tiers is straightforward. When a consumer purchases ground flaxseed and eats it raw — sprinkled on yoghurt or blended into a smoothie — the bioavailability of HCN is substantially higher than when whole seeds pass through the digestive system largely intact. Heat treatment during cooking or baking further reduces HCN through volatilisation.

In the animal feed sector, Directive 2002/32/EC sets a separate maximum of 250 mg/kg for HCN in feed materials containing linseed. EFSA has also evaluated decontamination processes — specifically enzymatic hydrolysis followed by evaporation — and confirmed that such methods can reduce HCN by approximately 90% while preserving the nutritional characteristics of the seed.

Why HCN Testing Is a Non-Negotiable for Buyers

For any business importing or exporting flaxseed into the European market — or into other regulated markets such as Japan, South Korea, and increasingly parts of South-East Asia — routine HCN testing is not optional. It is a core quality parameter alongside moisture, oil content, impurities, and pesticide residue levels.

Cultivar variability

Two lots of flaxseed harvested in the same region during the same season can differ by 100 mg/kg or more in HCN content purely based on the cultivar grown. Buyers who do not specify or verify cultivar characteristics risk receiving product that is technically non-compliant upon arrival.

Processing sensitivity

Grinding or milling flaxseed increases HCN bioavailability dramatically. Sweden's national food administration has specifically advised against consuming crushed or milled flaxseed without heat treatment. A lot testing at 180 mg/kg in whole-seed form may be perfectly legal as a bulk ingredient, but would fail the 150 mg/kg consumer-facing limit the moment it is milled and packaged for retail.

Market access

Japan and South Korea maintain particularly strict regulations on cyanogenic glycoside content in imported food products. Flaxseed intended for these markets may require processing or treatment to deplete CG levels before it can clear customs.

Acute toxicity thresholds

While the human body can detoxify modest amounts of cyanide — converting it to thiocyanate for urinary excretion — EFSA has noted that a single serving of flaxseed from a high-CG batch could, in theory, exceed the acute reference dose. This is precisely why the regulation exists.

How Processing Reduces HCN Content

Multiple processing methods have been shown to significantly reduce HCN in flaxseed. For buyers evaluating processed flaxseed or considering value-added products, the effectiveness of each method is worth understanding:

Processing Method
HCN Reduction
Extrusion cooking (barrel temp >140°C)
up to 89%
Microwave roasting
~83%
Lactic acid fermentation (Lactobacillaceae, 48 h)
< detection limit
Repeated pelleting (6 passes)
~55%
Autoclaving (steam-pressure treatment)
~30%

Lactic acid fermentation holds particular promise for producers targeting Japanese and South Korean markets — it reduces cyanogenic glycosides below detection limits within 48 hours while preserving oil content, lignan levels, and the fatty acid profile. The common thread across all effective methods is either enzymatic degradation followed by HCN removal, or direct thermal destruction of the precursor compounds.

What This Means for Sourcing from Bulgaria

Bulgaria is an established origin for high-quality flaxseed production, benefiting from suitable continental climate conditions and a well-developed agricultural export infrastructure. Bulgarian-origin flaxseed routinely undergoes laboratory testing for HCN content as part of standard export documentation, alongside Certificates of Analysis covering moisture, oil content, acid value, peroxide value, and pesticide residue compliance under EU Regulation (EC) No 396/2005.

For B2B buyers, sourcing from a Bulgarian exporter with direct access to accredited testing facilities means that HCN compliance can be verified at origin — before the product ships — rather than discovered as a problem at the port of destination. Pre-shipment Certificates of Analysis that include HCN testing provide documentary assurance that the lot meets the applicable regulatory threshold, whether that is the 250 mg/kg bulk limit or the 150 mg/kg consumer-facing limit.

Key Takeaways for Flaxseed Buyers

Specify HCN limits in contracts

Include the applicable threshold (150 mg/kg or 250 mg/kg) explicitly in purchase agreements.

Require pre-shipment HCN testing

Verify compliance at origin before the product ships — not at the port of destination.

Know the two EU thresholds

≤ 250 mg/kg for bulk/industrial; ≤ 150 mg/kg for consumer-facing product. The difference matters for intended use.

Demand complete analytical documentation

CoA with HCN result, phytosanitary certificate, certificate of origin, multi-residue pesticide analysis — all standard.

Account for processing in your limits

A lot compliant in whole-seed form may breach consumer limits after milling. Plan for downstream processing.

Source from EU-compliant origins

EU-grown flaxseed (including Bulgaria) is produced under EU plant protection rules, reducing pesticide MRL exceedance risk.

HCN content in flaxseed is not a reason to avoid the product. It is a reason to source it properly. The buyers who manage this parameter well are the ones who specify HCN limits in their purchase contracts, verify compliance through pre-shipment testing, understand the difference between the two regulatory thresholds, and work with suppliers who can provide complete analytical documentation as standard.
